The Long Game

Chapter 1: Null Pointer

Published March 2026  · 

The year was 2026, and Leo’s life was a monument to quiet competence. He was a senior data architect for a company that built the digital scaffolding for other companies. His work was invisible, essential, and flawless. His apartment was a minimalist’s dream in a refurbished factory in Antwerp: a place for everything, and everything in its place. His social life was a scheduled rotation of a Thursday beer with two colleagues, a monthly phone call with his mother in Bruges, and a meticulously planned solo hiking trip every autumn.

He was thirty-eight, healthy, and utterly, soul-crushingly content with the absence of surprise.

On a Tuesday in late March, the surprise arrived. It was a heart attack. Not the dramatic, cinematic kind with chest-grabbing and a fall to the floor. It was a slow, suffocating pressure, a feeling of a great hand squeezing the logic out of his world. He was at his standing desk, staring at a complex entity-relationship diagram, when a wave of nausea washed over him. His last conscious thought wasn’t of a lost love or a missed opportunity. It was, with a flicker of annoyance, I haven’t finalized the backup protocol for the Q3 migration.

Then, there was nothing.

✦   ✦   ✦

Consciousness returned not as a light, but as a sensation of being stretched. Like taffy. Like a thought being pulled across an infinite distance. He became aware of himself as a point of awareness, adrift in a silent, star-dusted void. Then, the void cracked open with a soundless roar, and he was falling.

He hit… nothing. He simply arrived.

His first sense was sight. The sky was not blue. It was the deep, bruised purple of twilight, but saturated with light from two moons. One was large and silver, hanging low like a celestial dinner plate. The other was smaller, tinged with ochre, trailing it like a loyal hound. They bathed the world below in a soft, binary glow.

Below. He looked down, and a primal panic, the last vestige of his physical body, seized him. There was no ground beneath his feet. There were no feet. He was floating, a thousand feet in the air, looking down at a sprawling, primeval forest. Rivers like silver veins cut through it, and in the distance, jagged mountain peaks clawed at the starry sky. He tried to move his arm, and a shimmer rippled through his field of view. He tried to look at his hand and saw only a faint, translucent distortion, a smudge in the moonlight.

He was a ghost. A consciousness without a corpse, adrift in an alien sky.

The panic began to curdle into a vast, existential loneliness. He was a data architect with no data, a man of systems in a world without any he could perceive. He was a null pointer, a floating point error in the universe. He tried to scream, but there was no mouth, no throat, only the intention of a scream that dissipated into the silent air.

Then, a sound. Not a sound he heard with ears, but one that was installed directly into the core of his awareness. It was crisp, synthetic, and horrifyingly familiar. It was the sound of a perfectly optimized system booting up.

✦   ✦   ✦

Ding.

[The Dungeon Creator System has been installed successfully.]

A translucent window, like a heads-up display from a video game he hadn’t played in fifteen years, materialized in his vision. It was crisp, blue-tinted, and utterly incongruous against the organic curves of the alien forest below.

He stared at it. His analytical mind, the only thing he’d brought with him from his old life, latched onto it like a drowning man to a lifeline. A system. A process. A set of parameters.

[User:] Leo Van den Berg (Deceased – Designation: Dungeon Master – Type: Unbound)
[Status:] Incorporeal | Mana: 100/100 | Core Integrity: 10%
[Available Resources:] None
[Available Terrain:] Unclaimed Airspace (Altitude: 1,200 ft)
[Primary Directive:] Establish Dungeon Heart.

Another window popped up beneath it, a tutorial.

Welcome, Dungeon Master.
You have been selected for a critical function: the creation and management of a dimensional nexus—commonly known as a Dungeon. Your corporeal form was incompatible with this plane of existence. This spectral state is temporary.
Your first objective is to select a location and manifest your Dungeon Heart. Once anchored, you will regain a localized physical form and begin resource management.

Leo floated in the silent air, the binary light of two moons washing over his invisible form. The existential terror was still there, a cold knot in his chest where his heart used to be. But now, it was being systematically overwritten by a different feeling.

He had spent his entire life organizing data, building systems, optimizing workflows. His life had been a quiet, uncelebrated masterpiece of order. And now, in death, the universe had finally given him a job description that matched his soul.

He looked down at the sprawling, untamed wilderness below. No spreadsheets. No quarterly reports. No backup protocols.

Just a blank canvas.

He focused on the translucent window. A new prompt appeared.

[Select Dungeon Core Location:]

A targeting reticle, invisible a moment before, pulsed in his vision, centered on a clearing in the forest below, shaped almost perfectly like a circle. A place for everything.

For the first time since waking up dead, Leo felt the ghost of a smile.

He willed the reticle to lock in place.

[Location Confirmed. Commencing Dungeon Heart Manifestation.]

A beam of pure, white light, no wider than his thumb, lanced from his spectral core down to the clearing. It struck the earth, and the ground didn’t break—it rearranged. Soil, stone, and ambient mana swirled in a miniature vortex, coalescing into a single, perfect, obsidian crystal that pulsed with a soft, rhythmic light. A heartbeat.

In that instant, Leo felt a tether. A connection. He was no longer just a ghost floating in the sky. He was the ghost of that crystal, of that clearing, of that nascent dungeon.

A new window appeared, this one sprawling with data.

[Dungeon Heart Active.]
[Name:] Unnamed Dungeon
[Rank:] F
[Mana Generation:] +1/hour
[Available Mana:] 0
[Core Integrity:] 50% (Stable)
[Available Floors:] 1/1
[Available Spawns:] None. Purchase Bestiary Blueprints from the Shop.
[Shop Tab:] Unlocked.

He felt a pull, a gentle gravity drawing him downwards. As he descended, the world came into sharper focus. He passed through the canopy of ancient trees, their leaves shimmering with a phosphorescent dew. He saw creatures—a six-legged stag drinking from a stream, a serpent with feathered wings coiled around a branch—and with a thought, he could analyze them.

[Greatwood Stag – Tier 1 Beast – Potential Dungeon Spawn: Yes]

He landed at the edge of the clearing, the obsidian crystal pulsing at its center. He was still incorporeal, but he felt more present now. Anchored. He looked at the crystal, his heart. Then he looked at the Shop tab.

He opened it. A list, organized by category, price, and tier, scrolled before his eyes. It was a database. A beautifully, intricately organized database of monsters, traps, treasures, and environmental modifiers.

Leo, the ghost of a data architect from Antwerp, stood in the moonlight of a world with two suns, staring at the ultimate system to build. A slow, deep sense of purpose settled over him, more profound than anything he’d ever felt in his living years.

He didn’t mourn his old life. He hadn’t been living it. He’d been curating it. For the first time, he had a project that mattered. A project with stakes. A project with a blank slate, a set of core mechanics, and an infinite potential for optimization.

He looked at his first floor, an empty dirt circle, and whispered into the silent night.

✦   ✦   ✦

“Alright,” he said, his voice a soft, spectral hum. “Let’s begin with the entryway. We’ll need a perimeter defense. Something cost-effective with a high psychological impact. A classic. Kobolds, perhaps. But we’ll need to optimize their patrol routes and trap synergy.”

He pulled up the blueprint for a Kobold Warrener, his mind already crunching the numbers. He had died of a heart attack at a standing desk. He had been reborn as a Dungeon Master.

And he was going to build the most meticulously designed, ruthlessly efficient, and utterly inescapable dungeon this world had ever seen. Not out of malice. But because, for the first time, he had a job that was truly his own.

The two moons gazed down, indifferent, as the first Kobold materialized in a puff of blue smoke, looked around with wide, reptilian eyes, and awaited its first command. The Long Game had truly begun.
